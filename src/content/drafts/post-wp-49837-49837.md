<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Taking an idea for a walk</h2>



<p>The other day, my colleague Steve and I were discussing book-direct pricing. He raised the question as to what a fair book-direct price would be based on.</p>



<p>Steve proposed that a <strong>fair book-direct price</strong> would be the price shown on Airbnb, Vrbo, or Booking.com &#8211; Minus their commission (or service fee, as they call it).</p>



<p>So, if your place is $115 a night on, say, Airbnb &#8211; You would charge $100 for a direct booking.</p>



<p>I think that this is how most hosts operate.</p>



<div class="wp-block-qubely-divider qubely-block-48cde5"><div class="qubely-block-divider "><div class="qubely-block-divider-style-dot"></div></div></div>

<p>I was playing devil&#8217;s advocate along the following lines&#8230;</p>



<ol>
<li>As Airbnb, Booking.com, and Vrbo are hugely successful, and they&#8217;re booking millions of nights at rates that include their commission, one could argue that these rates are, in fact, the market rate. </li>
</ol>



<p>Using the example above, the majority of guests are paying the $115 nightly rate, so wouldn&#8217;t it be fair to charge the same? </p>



<p><strong>Why would any of us charge less than market rate?</strong></p>



<p>2. When it comes to hosting, direct bookings are more work. Quotes need to be made, emails replied to, invoices sent, payments taken, etc., so it wouldn&#8217;t it be more than fair to charge the same as the online travel agencies.</p>



<p>3. With a little lateral thinking or creative accounting, you could get very clever and set yourself apart from your competition. If you are charging 15% above your base rate, you have money that could be used to buy welcome gifts or other &#8216;bonus&#8217; items for your guests. </p>



<p>If the average high season week books at $1,000, and you spent $100 on guest/bonus incentives, you would still be $50 better off than dropping the price by 15% in the first place.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-pullquote"><blockquote><p>Most hosts charge less for direct bookings.<br>Why not charge the same as the OTA and give the guest more?</p></blockquote></figure>



<p>You could offer any of the following&#8230;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Free damage deposit insurance. </h2>



<p>You can purchase damage deposit insurance for between 59 and 99 dollars, depending on the amount of coverage you opt for.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Policy options&nbsp;</strong></h4>



<ul>
<li>$59 for $1500 coverage&nbsp;</li>



<li>$79 for $3000 coverage&nbsp;</li>



<li>$99 for $5000 coverage </li>
</ul>



<p>This could be a great freebie, as asking for a damage deposit can be a barrier to direct booking. It also gives you peace of mind regarding accidental damage.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><br>Fresh flowers and local produce on arrival. </h2>



<p>We all know that first impressions count, so why not up your game and give guests who book directly some fresh flowers and a hamper of locally sourced goodies (or something similar).</p>



<p>I USED TO DO THIS when I was managing my properties, and it went down a storm. It builds a great guest/host relationship straight from the get-go. </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><br>$100 voucher for a local restaurant, boat trip, or something similar.</h2>



<p>This is another great gift for both you and those guests who book directly. They get a memorable freebie, and you get to share with the local business community. Alternatively, you could negotiate discounts for local attractions and provide vouchers for zoos, amusement parks, museums, etc.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Car hire or bicycle hire</h2>



<p>If you are in a fly to location, a voucher toward car or bicycle hire would be appreciated by your guests. This would help guests get out and about and explore the local area. </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Local maps and guides</h2>



<p>You could provide maps and local guides with a curated list of recommended places to see and attractions to visit. Shared inside knowledge goes a long way and guests appreciate insider tips more than anything else.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><br>Provide a beach basket or picnic basket for guest use</h2>



<p>Think about providing beach towels, buckets and spades for the kids, a beach ball, and a frisbee (all in a beach bag)  will save guests from having to fork out money and will be an instant hit if your property is located near the sea.</p>



<p>Likewise, if you are in a rural location, a picnic basket and directions to a little-known scenic location will go down a storm.</p>

<p>If your guests have a great experience when staying with you, they are far more likely to leave great reviews, so this is an added bonus when providing any of the above freebies. It&#8217;s a win-win situation.<br></p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">What you do is up to you.</h2>



<p>At the end of the day, there&#8217;s no right or wrong way of setting your direct booking pricing. This article isn&#8217;t about what you should do &#8211; it&#8217;s just a discussion covering different pricing methods.</p>



<p>The big brands have increased the price of our rentals (and our competition&#8217;s rentals), and that&#8217;s to our advantage. We can either promote ourselves as a cheaper option or offer our properties as a higher value proposition. </p>
