<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Secret #2</h3>



<p><strong>Some hosts don&#8217;t really want Airbnb bookings.</strong></p>



<p>So, I hear you ask, why would a host list on Airbnb if they don&#8217;t want bookings from Airbnb?<br>There are a few reasons for this&#8230;<br><br><strong>Firstly</strong>, the explosion of new properties to the vacation rental sector have resulted in an over saturation of the marketplace. Bookings are harder to get, so hosts have been forced to spread their marketing net and they need to list their rentals on multiple sites for more exposure.<br><br><strong>Secondly</strong>, the demographic of guests differs from platform to platform.<br>51% of Airbnb guests are under the age of 34 and 15% are aged 15 &#8211; 24.<br><br>Many hosts, particularly in the US, won&#8217;t allow guests under the age of 25 as they worry about parties and or damage to the property.<br>This is more common in high end homes.<br>BUT, you can&#8217;t be ageist on Airbnb, so, if you list your property anyone can book it.<br><br>So, they hike their prices to offset these potential problems and the possible costs of repairs or the loss of income from cancelling bookings while repairs are being made.</p>



<p><strong>Thirdly</strong>, hosts don&#8217;t trust the listing sites when it comes to cancellations.<br>Airbnb have a history of siding with the traveller when it comes to cancellations. </p>



<p>From a travellers perspective, that seems fair. If you can&#8217;t go on vacation for whatever reason, why should you pay? Surely you should get your money back!<br><br>But if a property is booked and the guest cancels at the last minute, the chances of the host rebooking their home is very slim and that&#8217;s lost income for the host. <br>Hosts are, after all, operating a business and they don&#8217;t like it when their rentals are sitting empty.<br><br> This really came to a head when Covid hit in 2020 and Airbnb refunded travellers all of their money, leaving hosts high and dry.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Let&#8217;s look at commissions</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Airbnb</h3>



<p>It&#8217;s no secret that Airbnb has been taking the world by storm over the past decade or so. The company has completely disrupted the travel industry, making it easier than ever for people to find accommodations for a short city break to a long haul vacation.</p>



<p>However, what many people don&#8217;t realise is that Airbnb charges commission on top of the rental price. This can add up to a LOT of money, especially if you are staying for a longer period of time.</p>



<p></p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">The Airbnb Commission Structure</h2>



<p>The following paragraph is from <a href="https://www.airbnb.co.uk/help/article/1857/airbnb-service-fees" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Airbnb&#8217;s own service fees page</a></p>



<p>&#8220;Most guest service fees are under 14.2% of the booking subtotal (nightly rate + cleaning fee + additional guest fee if applicable – excluding Airbnb fees and taxes). This fee varies based on a variety of factors and is shown during checkout before you book so you know what to expect&#8221;.</p>



<p>This may not seem like a lot, but it can add up quickly, especially if you are staying for a week or more. These commissions can become huge when booking luxury accommodation.</p>
