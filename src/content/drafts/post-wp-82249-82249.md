<p class="has-larken-font-family has-text-xxl-font-size">Embark on a journey through Japan with Chris Broad, the visionary mind behind the renowned YouTube channel, <a href="https://www.youtube.com/@AbroadinJapan" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Abroad in Japan</a>, and celebrated author on the Sunday Times bestseller list. From his unexpected entry into the Japan Exchange Teaching (JET) program to living a decade in this captivating country, Chris shares exclusive experiences and invaluable travel insights.</p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity" />
<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-who-is-chris-broad-and-how-did-you-end-up-in-japan">Who is Chris Broad and how did you end up in Japan?</h2>
<div class="wp-block-group is-nowrap is-layout-flex wp-container-1 wp-block-group-is-layout-flex">
<p>Growing up I always harboured a dream of living and working overseas while travelling the world. During a flight on route to see a friend in France, a chance encounter with a middle aged couple, whose daughter was teaching in Japan, led me to discover the Japan Exchange Teaching (JET) programme and the idea of living in Japan seemed almost too good to be true. I’d never considered Japan, assuming it’d cost too much to move to the country, but JET seemed to make it possible. I arrived in the summer of 2012 and I’ve never left!</p>
</div>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Congratulations on your Sunday Times bestseller, ‘Abroad in Japan.’ What can readers expect when picking up your <a href="https://www.waterstones.com/book/abroad-in-japan/chris-broad/9781787637078" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">book</a>? </h2>
<p>The Abroad in Japan book covers the entire journey of living 10 years in Japan; from arriving with no knowledge of Japanese and being a fairly clueless and underprepared English teacher, to experiencing huge earthquakes, North Korean missile incidents and getting to produce documentaries alongside incredible people such as the actor Ken Watanabe. It’s been quite the ride living in Japan and I had a lot of anxiety around the decision to move here, given the lack of Japanese knowledge, the absence of any family or even friends waiting for me on the far side of the world. It terrifies me that I nearly gave in to fear and didn’t move to Japan. If the book does one thing above all, I hope it’ll be to convince younger folks to consider living overseas or pursuing a daunting dream they may have. If I can do this, anyone one can!</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Japan is on many of our travellers’ bucket list. What’s the first thing people always ask you about living in Japan?</h2>
<p>I’m always asked where to go and what to do! It’s a question I struggle to answer as we all expect different things from our travels. But on your first trip to Japan you’d do well to get a balance between the obvious and essential destinations like <a href="https://www.skyscanner.net/transport/flights/uk/tyoa/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Tokyo</a> and Kyoto, while exploring less travelled regions such as central Japan and Tohoku in the north. Away from the crowds of Tokyo and Kyoto is when you’ll be able to have a moment to yourself and truly appreciate the beauty of Japan.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Is there anything you miss about your hometown? </h2>
<p>While I miss friends, family and British pub culture, the thrill of living in unfamiliar territory and learning new things every single day has always overruled any nostalgia. Still, I do my best to visit the UK once a year, particularly for Christmas. Christmas in Japan doesn’t quite cut it!</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Have you discovered any hidden gems? Maybe there’s an underrated place you’d recommend visiting? </h2>
<p>I’ve been extremely lucky to visit every single one of Japan’s 47 prefectures. A few underrated hidden gems include the mountainous prefecture of Yamagata, the culture rich northern region of Aomori, famed for cider and tuna, and the Setouchi inland sea. Rent a bike and cycle across the Shimanami Kaido cycle route from Hiroshima to Matsuyama and you’ll experience the most jaw dropping views across the chain of tiny islands that stretch the inland sea.</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">What tips would you give to someone visiting Japan for the first time? Any dos or don’ts to consider? </h2>
<p>Honestly, the only time I’ve seen anyone in Japan lose their mind with regards to etiquette was when a friend accidentally left his shoes on indoors! Naturally, they’re dirty and can cause damage on delicate tatami floors so don’t forget to remove them. Above all, just be mindful of how noisy you’re being in public; trains and buses in Japan are typically fairly quiet, like riding some sort of mobile library. Do the courtesy of keeping your voice down as half the commuters tend to be asleep after a long day.</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">How is Japanese culture different to Western culture? Are there any common misconceptions? Has anything surprised you? </h2>
<p>In ten years I’ve never witnessed a violent altercation or barely even an argument. Society here is very averse to conflict and does an incredible job keeping a sense of order, perhaps that’s the most different aspect to western culture. It has its downsides though; getting an opinion out of someone who isn’t a close friend can be fairly difficult. People would rather avoid stating their position by giving non-committal responses. For example, in my teaching days my colleagues often wouldn’t feel comfortable giving direct feedback on things like lesson plans. Constructive criticism is a great way to learn and improve so I always welcomed their comments, however politely shared!</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">What is your most memorable experience in Japan? Tell us a little about why that sticks out. </h2>
<p>Learning the Japanese language has been the most rewarding aspect of living in Japan. It’s not been an easy journey and it takes a lot of work. But after two years I was able to deliver a 15 minute speech in Japanese from memory, in a way I would never have dreamed possible years earlier – especially as someone who was bloody awful at German and Spanish at secondary school. It made me realise that I was far more capable than I realised.</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">What’s the most mind-blowing fact about Japan?</h2>
<p>The first Japanese Shinkansen bullet train began service in 1964, decades ahead of the UK. When it comes to infrastructure and mass transit, Japan makes a laughing stock out of most of the world.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">As someone who’s lived there for over 10 years, what’s the best time of year to visit Japan in your opinion? </h2>
<p>In my view Autumn, around the end of October, when the temperature cools and the trees burst into colour. Cherry Blossom season is great, but short-lived and extremely busy.</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">When is Cherry Blossom season in Japan? We’d love to know if you’ve got anything planned for next year’s festivities. </h2>
<p>It depends on where you are in the country! <a href="https://n-kishou.com/corp/news-contents/sakura/index_en.html">Japan has a weather map just for cherry blossoms</a> and it usually kicks off in Kyushu (south Japan) in mid March and snakes its way up the country reaching Hokkaido in May. If you’re in Tokyo, the first week of April is the best time to come.</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">We’ve noticed a spike in travellers searching for information on Japan’s bullet trains. Is this the best way to get around the country? </h2>
<p>Bullet trains are great for travelling between Tokyo and the region of Kansai with cities like Kyoto and Osaka. But often domestic flights are cheaper and more convenient for most of the country. If you’re visiting cities such as <a href="https://www.skyscanner.net/transport/flights/tyoa/fuk/">Fukuoka</a>, <a href="https://www.skyscanner.net/transport/flights/tyoa/spka">Sapporo</a> or even <a href="https://www.skyscanner.net/transport/flights/tyoa/hij/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Hiroshima</a> from Tokyo, don’t rule it out. It’ll save you a lot of time and money potentially.</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Travel hacks are a big topic on Skyscanner’s social platforms. Any hacks or other travel tips you could share that you’ve found especially useful?</h2>
<p>Restaurants across Japan are practically empty from 5pm to 6:30pm. However, by 7pm they’re completely full. I don’t know how or why – perhaps most folks finish work at 6:30 – but if I’m going out for dinner, if I’m not in a restaurant by 7pm in a major city, I essentially give up as the odds are they’ll be full. Get in any time from 5pm to 6:30pm and you’ll beat the crowds.</p>
