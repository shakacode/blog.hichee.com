<p>I<a href="https://blog.hichee.com/why-youll-be-750-times-better-off-running-an-airbnb-than-a-vacation-rental-or-holiday-let/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">f you missed it, you can read it here</a>.</p>



<p>I&#8217;d like to say a big thanks to those who read it, especially those who commented, because your comments certainly added to the conversation. </p>



<p>This short article looks at some of the discussion raised via your thoughts and comments.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Why has this happened?</h2>



<p>One of the most interesting comments came from <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/vanessa-humes-549b528/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Vanessa Humes</a>. She said&#8230;</p>



<figure class="wp-block-pullquote"><blockquote><p>There’s a large awareness to the brand Airbnb and it’s even taken over the identity because —- well to be honest we can’t figure out what we are—- vacation rentals, short term rentals, cottage rentals, holiday rentals etc etc. For lack of a better term, the public uses Airbnb and I think that’s the main point of the article. Something to think about for sure!</p><cite>Vanessa Humes</cite></blockquote></figure>



<p>This is spot on. In fact, the descriptive list of our properties goes on and on. It includes villa rentals, condos, apartments, glamping, caravan rentals, fincas, ski chalet rentals, tiny home rentals,  gites, cabins, boats, STRs, and more.<br></p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-color" style="color:#f70404">Airbnb has simply become a collective term for what we all do. </h3>



<div class="wp-block-qubely-divider qubely-block-d6dc3c"><div class="qubely-block-divider "><div class="qubely-block-divider-style-dot"></div></div></div>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">My examples were misleading.</h2>



<p>Some people questioned my search data, stating that people don&#8217;t search for the terms I cited. </p>



<p>That&#8217;s 100% true, but I couldn&#8217;t capture all the place names people search for, so I decided to show the trend instead of the details.</p>



<p>For example, Marc Figueras said&#8230;</p>



<figure class="wp-block-pullquote"><blockquote><p>Nice article. But your search data exercise is totally biased because nobody searches for a place to stay witout specifying a location.<br><br>Most guests who end up booking a VR/Airbnb/Apartment/STR/etc search &#8220;apartment in barcelona&#8221; which I&#8217;m sure gets more searches than Airbnb Barcelona. So, as much as Airbnb is a common word, it&#8217;s still less common than &#8220;apartment&#8221;. Otherwise Airbnb would have 99% of the market, and it doesn&#8217;t.</p><cite><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/ACoAAAksbU8Bb5t6Q1mfwJGeG7YL_8uTo6qORdI">Marc Figueras</a></cite></blockquote></figure>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-color" style="color:#fa0505">That&#8217;s a good point, but Airbnb Barcelona was searched for <strong>129 times more</strong> than Apartment in Barcelona, last month. 18.1k to 140. </h3>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Organic search vs. Overall traffic</h2>



<p>Marc&#8217;s other point was that, looking at my numbers, Airbnb would have 99% of the traffic, and they don&#8217;t. This is correct.</p>



<p>Many other factors are coming into play when looking at overall traffic. </p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Paid traffic</h4>



<p>Both Expedia (including Expedia&#8217;s Vrbo) and Booking.com spend over 5 billion a year (each) on paid traffic via Adsense. This level of advertising brings in a lot of traffic and bookings to both companies.</p>



<p>As an example, if someone searches for Airbnb Paris or Airbnb Orlando &#8211; they will see paid Google results from Booking.com. </p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Other marketing channels</h4>



<p>Expedia, Vrbo, and Booking.com also run television ad campaigns and have established brand awareness. These factors will bring direct traffic to their sites, <strong>as will their mobile apps</strong>.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">The radical book-direct movement</h2>



<p>Some owners, hosts, and managers held a much stronger view against rebranding their businesses.</p>



<p>Some won&#8217;t even mention the Airbnb name in something akin to Macbeth or Voldemort (as if it will bring bad luck or invoke some sort of taboo curse). </p>



<p>Others thought it went against &#8216;True book-direct ethics&#8217; or words to that effect. </p>



<p>I was one of the first proponents of people and companies actively seeking direct bookings, and in my opinion, all direct bookings are true direct bookings. If referring to my property as an Airbnb brings me more direct bookings, count me in. </p>



<p>As one comment put it, </p>



<figure class="wp-block-pullquote"><blockquote><p>&#8220;The public can call it anything they like &#8211; As Long as they book.&#8221; </p></blockquote></figure>



<p>Some of the more anti-Airbnb hosts suggested they would brand themselves as &#8216;Not an Airbnb&#8217;.</p>



<p> I initially thought this was quite a clever way of introducing the word Airbnb into their marketing. But on reflection, I&#8217;m not so sure. As I said in the original article, people are searching for an Airbnb, so saying that you are an Airbnb must help with conversions more than saying that you&#8217;re not an Airbnb.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">All publicity&#8230;</h2>



<p>Some hosts believe that any mention of Airbnb would harm the book-direct movement. Some said that any time we include the word AirBnb in our listings, website, FB pages, etc., we are, in effect, &#8220;promoting&#8221; and advertising for AirBnb. </p>



<p>This may be true to some degree, but if you search for the word Airbnb, Google returns over a third of a billion results, so a few more references won&#8217;t have any impact of note. </p>



<p>The horse has already bolted.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">At the end of the day&#8230;</h2>



<p>I wrote the original article because the data shows overwhelming evidence that the public uses the word Airbnb to a degree that can&#8217;t be ignored. I think that deep down, most of us already knew that, but the numbers really hammer it home.</p>



<p>I&#8217;m not suggesting that if you rebrand as an Airbnb, you will find your website showing up on the first page of Google. Google is smarter than all of us. It uses semantic search &#8211; so it already equates Airbnbs with vacation rentals, holiday homes, et al., in the same way that it brackets churches, cathedrals, and chapels together. <br><br>This is just about aligning our marketing with our potential and fee-paying guest&#8217;s vocabulary. If you want to convert lookers into bookers, it&#8217;s best to do so using their own language.<br><br></p>
