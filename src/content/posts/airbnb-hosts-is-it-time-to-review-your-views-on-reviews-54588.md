<p>Just a short post today.<br><br>I just came across a post on Facebook. It was from a host who had just received a 4-star review from a guest. This host was, and I quote, &#8216;Livid.&#8217;<br><br>TBH, I&#8217;ve seen hundreds, if not thousands of these type of posts over the years.<br><br>That got me thinking.<br><br>I wondered why hosts think they should get 5-star reviews &#8211; every time? </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Comparing Apples to Apples</h2>



<p><br>So, I did some digging to put things into context.</p>



<p>I searched for the most bought items on Amazon.<br><br>I don&#8217;t know how many people have bought <a href="https://www.amazon.com/Apple-MWP22AM-A-cr-AirPods-Renewed/dp/B0828BJGD2/ref=sr_1_2?keywords=top%2Bselling%2Bproducts&amp;qid=1695221906&amp;sr=8-2&amp;th=1" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Apple AirPods Pro</a>, but 18,760 people have left reviews &#8211; So a LOT of people have bought these.<br><br>Only 73% of reviewers left 5-star reviews. Believe it or not, 10% of reviewers left a 1-star review. Overall, the product has a 4.3 out of 5 rating. <br>BUT THAT ISN&#8217;T STOPPING PEOPLE BUYING THEM.<br><br>I also looked at <a href="https://www.amazon.com/2021-Apple-10-2-inch-Wi-Fi-256GB/dp/B09G91TLNJ/ref=sr_1_8?crid=3H3LGPCPCXN0N&amp;keywords=IPAD&amp;qid=1695223340&amp;sprefix=ipad%2Caps%2C321&amp;sr=8-8&amp;th=1" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">this iPad</a> &#8211; Say what you like about Apple products, love them or hate them, you have to admit that the build quality is top-notch. You know what you are getting.<br><br>But, of the whopping 134,000 reviews, 9% of people gave it a 4-star review, That&#8217;s over 12,000 4-star reviews. Another 4% of people gave it less than 4-stars.<br><br>Do any of us here believe that Apple gives a flying fig about those 12,000 4-star reviews? </p>



<p>Overall, this iPad has a 4.8 out of 5 rating. 4.8 is a good score, isn&#8217;t it? After all, you can&#8217;t please all of the people all of the time. </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Digging a little deeper, I discovered a big spoonful of irony.</h2>



<p><br><a href="https://www.trustpilot.com/review/www.airbnb.com" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Airbnb&#8217;s own rating on TrustPilot</a> is a woeful 1.3 out of 5. Most of the 11,600 reviewers have trash-talked the company &#8211; And some. It&#8217;s as bad as it gets &#8211; BUT Airbnb takes over a million bookings a night. Their poor rating doesn&#8217;t seem to be affecting them at all.</p>



<p>I understand how a 4-star review can be disappointing for hard-working, eager-to-please hosts &#8211; But we need to be realistic. We&#8217;re dealing with the public; we all know they can be difficult to please. </p>



<p>I play golf in my spare time, and there&#8217;s a very interesting book called <strong>Golf is not a game of perfect</strong>. The idea behind the book is to reduce expectations, as this leads to happier, less frustrating golf.<br><br>Maybe our hosting mantra should be: <strong>Guests are not a game of perfect</strong>. <br><br>They can be fickle, picky, moody, and at times, a real pain. But, luckily enough, most are lovely.<br><br>So, next time you receive anything other than a 5-star review, just make yourself a cup of tea or a large gin and tonic and remember that these ratings don&#8217;t really matter. They aren&#8217;t going to stop people from booking.</p>



<p>Some would argue that the odd 4-star review adds authenticity. Some say that the odd 1 or 2-star review just shows that particular guest in a bad light.<br><br>As long as most of your reviews are 5-stars, you&#8217;ll be just fine.<br><br>Either way, your guest reviews will never be as bad as Airbnb&#8217;s own reviews, and that&#8217;s just nice to know.  <br><br><br></p>
