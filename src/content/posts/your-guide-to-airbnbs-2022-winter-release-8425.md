<p>Protecting rental properties from damage is typically top of mind for all property managers/hosts. Knowing who your guests are and how they plan to use your rental is incredibly important, especially as winter party season is getting closer.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Queue Airbnb’s<a href="https://www.airbnb.com/2022-winter"> 2022 Winter release</a>, which was announced last week (November 16th)!</p>



<p>This release was well overdue; #Airbnbust has been trending across social media platforms for many months, so hosts will welcome the improvements with open arms. But, while Airbnb markets this as a “big improvement” (there are some improvements), many are actually things that have existed for a while.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p><strong>So the question is, is this some marketing ploy for Airbnb to<a href="https://skift.com/2022/11/16/airbnb-to-ease-host-sign-up-process-add-more-search-categories/"> grow its supply and attract more hosts</a>, or will it benefit current Airbnb hosts?&nbsp;</strong></p>



<p>In this article, we’ll cover everything you need to know about Airbnb’s 2022 winter release from a risk management perspective, why it’s important and what it includes.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Round-up of Airbnb’s 10 major upgrades:</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">1 – Aircover increased to $3 million</h3>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Is it new to Airbnb? <strong><span class="has-inline-color has-vivid-red-color">No</span></strong></h4>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Is it an improvement? <strong><span class="has-inline-color has-vivid-green-cyan-color">Yes</span></strong></h4>



<p>Damage protection is, of course, a necessity for all property managers and hosts. <strong>Making sure you are covered for guest damages while renting your short-term rentals is a no-brainer.&nbsp;</strong></p>



<p>In 2021 Airbnb introduced Aircover (up to $1 million in damage protection) to inspire new hosts to join the platform in unprecedented times.&nbsp;</p>



<p><strong>Now</strong>: Airbnb has increased Aircover protection to $3 million, and now includes:</p>



<ul>
<li><em>Protections for cars and boats parked at your home</em></li>



<li><em>Expanded coverage on art and valuables.</em></li>
</ul>



<p>An increase in the coverage is great to see; <strong>however, it seems likely that this increase was implemented to try and outperform Airbnb’s biggest competitors</strong> – Booking.com and Vrbo, who have recently introduced $1 million in damage protection for all their hosts/property managers who list with them.&nbsp;</p>



<p>While the increase from one million to three million is an improvement, realistically will this be an improvement for property managers and hosts? Likely, no!</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">2- Guest Identity Verification</h3>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Is it new to Airbnb? <strong><span class="has-inline-color has-vivid-red-color">No</span></strong></h4>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Is it an improvement? <strong><span class="has-inline-color has-vivid-green-cyan-color">Yes</span></strong></h4>



<p>Inviting a wide range of guests, especially big OTAs, into your properties makes background checks and <a href="https://superhog.com/guest-screening-for-vacation-rentals/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">guest screening</a> even more important. <strong>As a responsible property manager/owner, you must ensure that guests are who they say they are, can be trusted to respect your rules, and are not liable to bring problems to your property.&nbsp;</strong></p>



<p>Without identity verification/screening, you are opening yourself up to major risks such as damage, fraud, and noise complaints, which is exactly why Airbnb needed to implement this improvement.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Identity verification has been present since Airbnb’s inception, but it was never at the forefront due to Airbnb’s guest-centric stance. Previously, Airbnb asked guests to verify their identity to check they are who they say they are; however, identity verification was not a part of the Airbnb signup process, meaning not all guests on Airbnb are verified.</p>



<p>In Airbnb’s 2022 winter release, the new <strong><em>“comprehensive verification system checks details such as name, address, government ID, and more to confirm the identity of guests who book on Airbnb”</em></strong><em>.</em></p>



<p>While this update is a step in the right direction and one we are incredibly excited to see go global in the spring of 2023, it does leave some questions unanswered. Is Airbnb’s identity verification really insufficient, and why should we trust them now if, it wasn’t working before?</p>



<p>Airbnb still has a long way to go to provide property managers and hosts with more transparency regarding identity verification and to explain how their tech works, especially as it is still incredibly easy for guests to book.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">3 – Reservation screening technology</h3>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Is it new to Airbnb? <strong><span class="has-inline-color has-vivid-red-color">No</span></strong></h4>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Is it an improvement? <strong><span class="has-inline-color has-vivid-green-cyan-color">Yes </span><em>and </em><span class="has-inline-color has-vivid-red-color">No</span></strong></h4>



<p>A property manager or host’s worst nightmare is having their properties trashed and neighbours complaining due to out-of-control parties.</p>



<p>Airbnb’s reservation screening AI <em><strong>‘technology looks at hundreds of factors to block bookings that may lead to unauthorised parties.’</strong> – Brian Chesky.&nbsp;</em></p>



<p><strong>Airbnb’s red flags include:&nbsp;</strong></p>



<ul>
<li><em>guests of a specific age group (below 25 years old) who live in the same area as your rental,</em></li>



<li><em>booking properties for 10+ people,</em></li>



<li><em>booking an entire home for a one-night stay,&nbsp;</em></li>



<li><em>guests who have no positive reviews.</em></li>
</ul>



<p>With the tightening of short-term rental regulations in the US and popular European cities, further improvements to the screening technology come as no surprise.</p>



<p><strong>It is important to note that this is not a new feature.</strong> Airbnb introduced this technology back in October 2020 to limit house parties and <a href="https://news.airbnb.com/airbnb-announces-global-party-ban/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">crack down on unauthorised gatherings</a>. <strong>The major improvement here is that it will be extended across more countries.</strong></p>



<p>While guest screening is great, Airbnb’s AI reservation screening has brought some questions from hosts, especially those with larger properties. Property managers are worried Airbnb AI bots will block booking requests that could have in fact been a very solid booking. <strong>“<em>relying on AI to screen that is very very scary, also I have many large homes that sleep 12-15 people, this part makes me very worried</em>”</strong> said John Hildebrand, Founder of HildeHomes, when speaking on Mark Simpson’s <a href="https://boostly.co.uk/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Boostly</a> <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5eBECpaVzJI" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Podcast</a>.</p>



<p>While this is a move in the right direction, <strong>it does leave the question of how much choice and control will remain with the host when it comes to accepting bookings.<br></strong><br>After all, the guests aren’t staying with Airbnb, they are staying with a “host”, and <strong>therefore the risk is still being taken by the hosts.</strong> So shouldn’t it be up to a host or property manager to decide whether or not they want to accept the booking?</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">4 – Easier to file a claim</h3>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Is it new to Airbnb? <strong><span class="has-inline-color has-vivid-red-color">No</span></strong></h4>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Is it an improvement? <strong><span class="has-inline-color has-vivid-green-cyan-color">Yes</span></strong></h4>



<p>This is a long-standing battle that hosts have requested, making the process to file a damage claim against guests as simple and the payout as quick as possible!&nbsp;</p>



<p>And it seems like Airbnb might have listened! In an interview with <a href="https://skift.com/2022/11/16/airbnb-to-ease-host-sign-up-process-add-more-search-categories/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Skift</a>, Catherine Powell, Global Head of Hosting, said Airbnb has reduced the<strong> <em>“steps to file a claim from the current 14 to 3. The process for getting compensated for a claim would remain an average of two weeks after the claim gets filed”. </em></strong>Music to the ears of many Airbnb hosts!</p>



<p>However, it has been made clear by hosts that <strong>it is not the process they do not like, but rather the fact decisions typically favour guests than hosts</strong>, and that Airbnb is notorious for taking the guest’s side, leaving hosts to deal with the damages themselves.</p>



<p>Let’s see what comes out of this one.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">5 – Ground rules for guests</h3>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Is it new to Airbnb? <strong><span class="has-inline-color has-vivid-red-color">No</span></strong></h4>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Is it an improvement? <strong><span class="has-inline-color has-vivid-green-cyan-color">Yes</span></strong></h4>



<p>Improvements that force guest accountability will be a win in our books!</p>



<p>It’s great to see that Airbnb is working towards building a more trusted relationship between hosts and guests by acknowledging the behaviours of both parties, and this has been well received by hosts.</p>



<p><em>“</em><strong><em>Finally, we will be able to educate the guests on what is responsible on the guest’s side when they’re booking an Airbnb… Lately, guests just think if they’re booking an Airbnb, they can get away with whatever they want. Whatever they want is always right, and us hosts have to do whatever it takes to make them happy. Otherwise, we’ll get a bad review.”</em></strong> – <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZH-j6pIghKc" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">John Hildebrand</a>, Founder of <a href="https://hildehomes.com/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">HildeHomes </a>and Airbnb Ambassador.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Airbnb had House Rules (text the hosts would write to inform guests of what was expected of them) but these rules were not enforced and often left hosts to deal with the consequences.</p>



<p>Ground Rules are an extra layer of protection on top of the House rules, so it will be interesting to see how the <em>‘</em><strong><em>new set of </em><a href="https://community.withairbnb.com/t5/Airbnb-updates/A-new-system-to-hold-guests-accountable/td-p/1712404" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><em>enforceable standards</em></a><em> that all guests must follow</em>’</strong> will play out.&nbsp;</p>



<p>The penalties for breaking the ground rules include:</p>



<ul>
<li>First a warning</li>



<li>A suspension for the guest if they continue to break them</li>



<li>Removal from Airbnb, if warranted</li>
</ul>



<p>Let’s see how Airbnb executes this!</p>



<p>We are optimistic and <strong>we hope the introduction will contribute to an increase in guest accountability and ultimately reduce the push and pull seen in guest verification.</strong></p>

<p>But does this feature really matter if your listing is instantly bookable (the default booking option for all listings on Airbnb)? <strong>Once again, it comes full circle in making sure you know who your guest is before taking the booking.&nbsp;</strong></p>
