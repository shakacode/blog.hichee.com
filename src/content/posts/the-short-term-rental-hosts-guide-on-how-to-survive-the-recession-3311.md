<p class="has-medium-font-size"><strong>The overall trend</strong></p>



<p>More and more travelers are looking to save money on their vacation/holiday rental accommodation. This behaviour is increasing and will accelerate as we head deeper into recession. </p>



<p>As people’s disposable income becomes stretched, they change their behaviour.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size"><strong>The things we buy.</strong></p>



<p>No matter what or where you sell your products or services they fall into one of four categories.</p>



<p>1. <strong>Essentials</strong><br>2. <strong>Treats</strong><br>3. <strong>Postponables</strong><br>4. <strong>Expendables</strong></p>



<p><strong>Essentials</strong> &#8211; Obviously, food, water, and heating (if you live in a colder climate) fall into the essentials category. But if you need to drive to work then a car and fuel are essential.&nbsp;Medicine is another that many can&#8217;t do without.</p>



<p><strong>Treats</strong> &#8211; Eating out, nights at the cinema or theatre, drinks at the pub, and Netflix subscriptions can be classed as treats.&nbsp;They aren&#8217;t essential but they make our lives better. <em>We all enjoy treats as and when we can afford them.</em></p>



<p><strong>Postponeables</strong> &#8211; Vacations and holidays are postponable, as are buying a new car or new clothes. Upgrading any appliances, home furnishings, or mobile phones are postponable and these transactions will be put off in times of financial hardship.</p>



<p><strong>Expendables</strong> &#8211; Things that we want but don’t actually need, fall into the expendable category. I may want a speed boat or a sports car but I don’t need either one.&nbsp;Those dreams are very much put on hold during a recession.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">How people behave</h2>



<p>Understanding how people react to a change in financial circumstances is key to getting our businesses through harder times.</p>



<p>Different people behave in different ways during a recession. This behaviour will depend on their financial circumstances and their nature.</p>



<p>Some will slam the brakes on their spending, while others will take a pained but patient approach. </p>



<p>The comfortably well-off won’t be as affected as those with less disposable income but that’s not to say that the well-off won’t be more careful when it comes to spending. </p>



<p>Others will take a live-for-today approach and carry on spending regardless.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-pullquote"><blockquote><p><strong>Overall, people tend to move up a category when money becomes tighter.&nbsp;</strong></p></blockquote></figure>



<p>It’s very important to understand how people in general and our potential guests, in particular, behave when they start to feel the pinch financially.</p>



<p>Even those who only shop in the essentials category at present may stop buying more expensive (mini-treat) groceries like organic fruit and vegetables. They may swap from branded goods like Kleenex to a supermarket&#8217;s own budget brand paper goods or shop for daily use items at budget outlets like Aldi or Lidl. I&#8217;ll come back to this later.</p>



<p>People buying in the other categories will also make spending adjustments</p>



<p>Let’s take the treats category as an example. If a couple eats out regularly, they will either eat out less or move up into the essentials category. They may still treat themselves to a nice meal and a glass or two of wine on the weekend but they will cook it themselves, at home, in order to save money.</p>



<p>The same goes for things that are postponable&#8230;</p>



<figure class="wp-block-pullquote"><blockquote><p>Many will postpone their vacation. That will come as no surprise as vacations fall into the postponable bracket but many will move up a category into the treats sector. </p><p>So, instead of booking a one or two-week fly-to trip, they are more likely to book a shorter weekend getaway at a nearer location.</p></blockquote></figure>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img decoding="async" width="940" height="788" src="https://blog.hichee.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/The-Short-Term-Rental-Hosts-Guide-On-How-To-Survive-The-Recession.jpg" alt="The Short-Term Rental Hosts Guide On How To Survive The Recession" class="wp-image-3499" srcset="https://blog.hichee.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/The-Short-Term-Rental-Hosts-Guide-On-How-To-Survive-The-Recession.jpg 940w, https://blog.hichee.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/The-Short-Term-Rental-Hosts-Guide-On-How-To-Survive-The-Recession-300x251.jpg 300w, https://blog.hichee.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/The-Short-Term-Rental-Hosts-Guide-On-How-To-Survive-The-Recession-768x644.jpg 768w" sizes="(max-width: 940px) 100vw, 940px" /></figure>



<p>They will also start paying a lot more attention to the overall cost of their trip. Overpaying for something falls into the expendable category and everyone except the exceptionally well-off will be leaving that category.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">How are travelers behaving?</h2>



<p>Here at HiChee, we can see that more and more potential guests are looking for alternative booking options where they may find lower prices for the same property, for the same dates, and for the same number of guests.</p>

<p>We know this because that&#8217;s how tens of thousands of potential guests come to our site every day and each and every one of them is looking to compare prices and find the best deal.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">The trouble with booking direct</h2>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img decoding="async" width="992" height="740" src="https://blog.hichee.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/Screenshot-2022-10-13-at-11.26.45.png" alt="" class="wp-image-3483" srcset="https://blog.hichee.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/Screenshot-2022-10-13-at-11.26.45.png 992w, https://blog.hichee.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/Screenshot-2022-10-13-at-11.26.45-300x224.png 300w, https://blog.hichee.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/Screenshot-2022-10-13-at-11.26.45-768x573.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 992px) 100vw, 992px" /></figure>



<p>As managers and hosts, we know that we&#8217;re not scammers and that what we are offering is legitimate, guest friendly and safe. But first time buyers don&#8217;t know these things and they, quite understandably, have doubts like,</p>



<p>Can I trust this person or company?<br>Is this a ghost rental?<br>Will I lose my money?</p>



<p>During a recession, when money is more precious, these doubts increase and form a bigger obstacle in the direct-booking process than normal. </p>



<p>By verifying your listing/s on HiChee you raise the trustometer and lower the fear factor that deters travelers from booking direct.<br><br>The potential guest can see that you are the same owner as on the listing on Airbnb, Booking, or Vrbo. They can also see that HiChee has verified you or your company, provided links to your own website and, better still, delivered a communication channel where they can ask questions and build a relationship with you before booking.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-pullquote"><blockquote><p>All of this builds trust and the more that the interested party trusts you, the more likely they are to book.</p></blockquote></figure>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Even if they don&#8217;t book direct </h2>



<p>Some travelers will always prefer to stay on brand and book through an OTA. After all, Airbnb offer <strong><a href="https://www.airbnb.com/aircover" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Aircover</a></strong>, Vrbo provide their <strong><a href="https://www.vrbo.com/l/travel-with-confidence/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Book with Confidence Guarantee</a></strong>, and Booking (.com) have very <strong>flexible cancellation policies</strong>.</p>



<p>Booking via these companies brings a certain peace of mind and some travelers will always display brand loyalty for these and other reasons.</p>



<p>BUT, maybe the traveler has found a place on Vrbo but it turns out that it&#8217;s cheaper to book through Airbnb or Booking (.com), or vice versa &#8211; that price saving may bring your property from over budget to within budget. </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Think local</h2>



<p>As people move off brand looking for better deals on their accommodation they will be carrying out more research at the pre booking stage.</p>



<p>Preempt this by carrying out your own research for your destination. Look for local listing sites that charge less commission that the big 3 travel brands. Seriously consider listing with those that are high in Google&#8217;s search results.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Conclusions</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Be agile. Get recession ready</h3>



<p>As we move deeper into recession and disposable income becomes a thing in short supply, people will have no choice but to postpone big ticket purchases like vacations/holidays.</p>



<p>This has already started to become an issue in some locations and will start to become much more apparent in the traditional January booking window when people are paying the price for Christmas spending and worrying about upcoming winter energy bills, rising inflation and heavier mortgage repayments.</p>



<p>Hosts and managers could be facing empty inboxes and equally empty calendars.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Short term stays will become more popular</h3>



<p><strong>Consider reducing your minimum night stays.</strong> As mentioned above, potential guests will change their behaviour and move into the treats category. They may not be able to warrant the expense of a 7-night stay but they could stretch to a 3-night getaway. </p>



<p>Of course, shorter stays bring logistical challenges. In particular, rescheduling cleaning rotas and increased cleaning fees on a per night basis, along with more meet and greets and changeovers but may be the only option as opposed to empty properties.</p>



<p>I&#8217;ve lost count of the times that I&#8217;ve heard hosts say that they would rather be empty than drop their price on a booking and that&#8217;s understandable when demand is high but that mindset may need to change as bookings become hard to come by.</p>



<p>Seriously consider yearly income over nightly rates. Bills still need to be paid, maintenance still needs to be carried out, and recessions don&#8217;t last forever. If short-term changes get you through hard times and keep you in the game that&#8217;s preferable to being hard-nosed and going bust.</p>



<p>Also bear in mind that travel, in itself, is becoming more expensive due to increasing fuel prices so people will look to take their holidays closer to home.</p>
